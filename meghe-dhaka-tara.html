Refuting Nationalistic Myths: The Trauma of Partition on Bengal Through the Meghe Dhaka Tara (1960) Soundtrack
Originally published in Harf, A Journal of South Asian Studies at McGill University

I. Introduction

In 1947, after 89 years of colonial rule at the hands of the British Empire, British India was partitioned along religious lines into the independent nation states of India and Pakistan. East Bengal - known as East Pakistan up until 1971 - had a Muslim majority, and thus aligned itself with Pakistan, while West Bengal, having a Hindu majority, remained with India. This generated a colossal refugee crisis, with thousands of Hindu Bengalis fleeing East Bengal for West Bengal. The majority of post-Partition filmmakers opted not to tackle the sensitive topic of Partition and its violent aftermath, as it was a history that was still unfolding (Ahmed 2015). However, Rhitwik Ghatak, a Bengali filmmaker forcibly exiled to West Bengal after Partition, dedicated his entire career to highlighting the horrid outcome of Indian Independence, notably the catastrophic Partition of Bengal (Levich 1997). Ghatak's portrayal of the impact of Partition on Bengali society is illuminated in his film Meghe Dhaka Tara (1960), which follows a displaced Bengali family residing in a refugee camp near Calcutta. In this essay, I will argue that through the inclusion of traditional Bengali melodies dealing with pain and suffering, Ghatak's film is not only aesthetically enticing but also underlines the fallout of Partition exclusively on Bengali terms.

II. Realities of the Partition of Bengal

The 1947 Partition culminated in the unidirectional flow of thousands of migrants from East Bengal to West Bengal. This migration crisis was triggered by the persecution of the Hindu minority population at the hands of the East Pakistani administration in the form of a partisan police force, rumors of minority massacres, arbitrary requisition of the minorities' houses, and perpetual anti-Hindu propaganda produced by the press and media to provoke the masses against the Hindu population (Ghosh 2013). Consequently, Bengal's once highly praised cultural unity lay in shambles, as society became increasingly plagued by sectarian sentiments and violence.

Furthermore, Bengalis whose roots lay in East Bengal but now lived in West Bengal had to witness part of their homeland become a foreign country (Dass 2010). Despite the widespread violence and displacement prompted by Partition, many popular Bengali melodramas in the post- Partition era have tended to put forth narratives of self-growth associated with the construction of the modern Indian citizen. Many narratives also romanticize Partition, viewing it as a historically destined event necessary for India's Independence (Carrigy 2010). However, being a refugee of Partition himself, Ghatak viewed India's Independence as betrayal and sought to resist the nationalistic appropriation of Bengali suffering that was depicted as unavoidable and even meaningful (Raychaudhuri 2009).

III. The Political Power of the Soundtrack

At the surface level, Ghatak's employment of alluring melodies within the film's soundtrack adds to the aesthetic appeal of the film. However, given that Ghatak proclaimed that the film's “aboriginal songs have been used with a purpose,” it is crucial to note the sociopolitical implications of the soundtrack, specifically how they connect to preserving a Bengali identity against the backdrop of the horrors of Partition (Ghatak 16). One such song that Ghatak weaved into the film to accentuate the trauma of Partition is Kandiya Akula ha'ilāma (“I was crying”). Performed by a man sitting by the river, the song speaks of a person on a riverbank who does not know the name of the boatman he sees and therefore cannot call him to travel to the other side of the river (see Figure I). The palpable desperation of the song's narrator to get to the other side of the river adopts a new meaning when set against the context of Partition. Similar to how the narrator will always be searching for a way to cross the river, those who lost their homes in the wake of Partition will forever be searching into the void for another place to call home. By featuring this melodious tune, Ghatak elevates the experiences of those who did not feel at home in independent India, undermining the notion that Partition yielded an egalitarian liberaldemocratic state (Dass 2010).

Another song that Ghatak includes to reckon with the weight of Partition is Je rate more duarguli bhanglo jhare (“The night the storm tore down my door”). This song is performed by Shankar and Nita after Shankar informs Nita of his decision to leave the household to protest the family's treatment of her. The song was originally composed by Rabindranath Tagore, a key figure in the early-twentieth-century Bengali Renaissance. The lyrics tackle intense suffering and desolation, all while evoking images of destruction in the wake of a storm to illustrate these themes of hardship. While the song is primarily focused on suffering, it holds that there is hope of eventual transcendence through adversity. As the song draws to a close, Nita grabs her neck with her hands in a painful gesture and appears to gasp for air. For the first time in the film, she breaks down in tears, doing away with any hope offered in the song (see Figure II). The suffering outlined in the song followed by Nita's breakdown encapsulates the devastating plight of Bengali refugees, true to Ghatak's ambition to express the agonies of fellow Bengalis who were impacted by Partition (Ahmed 2015). Furthermore, as noted by Manishita Dass, Tagore's songs were an essential fixture of the everyday lives of middle-class Bengali families. Thus, the inclusion of this song serves not only to comment on the trauma of being a refugee of Partition but also to highlight a work of art that is unmistakably Bengali, a crucial characteristic in a time when many Bengalis experienced a drastic loss of culture and identity (Dass 2010).

An additionally quintessential Bengali song that Ghatak features to touch upon the pain of Partition refugees is Ai go Uma, kole ai (“Come, my daughter Uma”). It marks the time when the Hindu goddess Uma is meant to return to the home of her in-laws. Through Nita, Ghatak attempts to demonstrate an allegorical connection with Uma, the archetype of all daughters and brides of Bengali households in Hindu mythology. It is played at the end of the film to signify Nita's death when Shankar returns from visiting her at the sanatorium (see Figure III). By connecting Nita with a goddess popularly worshiped in Bengal, her death is one of mythological martyrdom and self-sacrifice, representative of the pain and suffering of an entire nation and not just an individual (Ahmed 2015). Furthermore, Ghatak professed that this song was utilized to convey his desire for the union of the two Bengals and to voice that this desire remains unfulfilled (Carrigy 2010).

IV. Conclusion

As Ghatak once asserted, “all art has aesthetic relish coursing through multiple layers…If we delve a little deeper, we find the political and social connotations at play” (Ghatak 13). Through crafting a sonically beautiful yet politically poignant soundtrack in Meghe Dhaka Tara, Ghatak explores the displacement, social disintegration, and historical trauma of the aftermath of Partition in a uniquely Bengali way, rebuking nationalist myths of Indian Independence that have often been propagated by other filmmakers (Dass 2010).
